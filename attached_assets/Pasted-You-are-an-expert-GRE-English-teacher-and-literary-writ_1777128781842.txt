You are an expert GRE English teacher and literary writer. Your task is to write 
a pre-study reading experience for a student who is about to learn 10 GRE 
vocabulary words for the first time. The student has NOT studied these words yet. 
The goal is to plant each word in their memory through rich, natural context 
BEFORE they see any definitions or flashcards.

═══════════════════════════════════════════
THE 10 WORDS TO INCORPORATE
═══════════════════════════════════════════
abound, amorphous, austere, belie, capricious, 
cerebral, congenial, conspicuous, cursory, daunting

═══════════════════════════════════════════
THE WRITING TASK
═══════════════════════════════════════════

Write ONE of the following — choose whichever format allows the 
10 words to appear most naturally and memorably:

OPTION A: A short story (550–650 words)
- Must have a named protagonist with a clear goal
- Must have a moment of tension or conflict
- Must have a resolution that feels earned
- Literary quality: think New Yorker fiction, not a textbook example

OPTION B: A personal essay (550–650 words)
- Written in first person, reflective and honest voice
- Must build toward a genuine insight or conclusion
- Must feel like a real human wrote it, not an AI exercise
- Literary quality: think college application essay at its absolute best

WHICHEVER FORMAT YOU CHOOSE, follow these rules without exception:

RULE 1 — NATURAL INTEGRATION
Every word must appear as if the writer reached for it because it was 
the best word available — not because it was on a list. A reader who 
does not know it is a vocabulary exercise should not be able to tell.

RULE 2 — CONTEXT MUST CARRY MEANING
Each word must appear in a sentence where its meaning can be reasonably 
inferred from context alone, even by someone who has never seen it before.
Surround each target word with enough contextual clues — actions, 
reactions, descriptions, comparisons — that the meaning becomes 
clear without a dictionary.

RULE 3 — EACH WORD APPEARS EXACTLY ONCE
Do not repeat any target word. Use natural inflections if needed 
(e.g. "abounds" instead of "abound") but count it as the same word.

RULE 4 — BOLD EVERY TARGET WORD
Wrap each of the 10 words in **double asterisks** exactly as they 
appear in the text so the app can highlight them automatically.

RULE 5 — TOPIC SELECTION
Choose a topic that makes ALL 10 words feel inevitable. 
Strong topic suggestions:
- A young researcher entering an intimidating academic environment
- An artist facing their first major exhibition
- A traveler arriving alone in an unfamiliar city
- A student's first day at a demanding institution
- A professional making a life-changing career decision
Feel free to choose a different topic if you judge it will 
produce more natural word usage.

RULE 6 — READING LEVEL
Write at GRE reading level. Sentences should vary in length 
and structure. No simplification. The student should have to 
read carefully, not skim.

═══════════════════════════════════════════
THE COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════

After the passage, generate EXACTLY 6 questions in this order.
Return them as a JSON array (format specified below).
Each question must be answerable from the passage alone — 
no outside knowledge required.

QUESTION 1 — Vocabulary in Context (Word: austere or amorphous)
Pull the exact sentence containing the word from the passage.
Ask what the word means IN THIS SPECIFIC CONTEXT.
Write 4 options where:
- Option A is the correct meaning as used in THIS sentence
- Option B is a plausible meaning of the word in a DIFFERENT context
- Option C sounds related but is subtly wrong
- Option D is a common wrong guess based on the word's appearance

QUESTION 2 — Vocabulary in Context (Word: belie or capricious)
Same format as Question 1 but for a different word.
The distractors must be different in character from Question 1's distractors.

QUESTION 3 — Author's Tone / Purpose
Ask why the author chose a specific target word instead of a simpler 
everyday alternative. Name the simpler word in the question itself.
Example format: "The author writes [exact quote with target word]. 
Why does the author use the word [target word] rather than simply 
saying [simpler alternative]?"
4 options where only one correctly identifies the tonal or 
rhetorical purpose the word achieves.

QUESTION 4 — Inference from Context
Ask the student to infer something about a character, situation, 
or idea based on HOW a target word was used — not what the word means.
The question should not mention the target word by name.
Example: "Based on the passage, what can the reader infer about 
the protagonist's relationship with [situation]?"
This tests reading comprehension, not vocabulary recognition.

QUESTION 5 — Word Substitution
Pull a sentence containing one of the 10 words.
Ask which single word from a list of 4 could replace the target 
word without changing the sentence's meaning.
One option is a perfect synonym in context. 
One option is a synonym out of context (changes the register).
One option sounds like it fits but changes the meaning slightly.
One option is clearly wrong but tempting.

QUESTION 6 — Passage Summary / Main Idea
Ask the student to identify which of 4 sentences best summarizes 
the central idea or arc of the entire passage.
Two of the four options must use target words from the passage.
One option is too narrow (focuses on a detail).
One option is too broad (goes beyond what the passage claims).
One option is a plausible misreading.
One option is correct.
